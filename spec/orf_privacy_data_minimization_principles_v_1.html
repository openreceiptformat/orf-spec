Introduction

The Open Receipt Format (ORF) is designed to enable interoperable, machine-readable digital receipts while respecting individual privacy, minimizing unnecessary data collection, and supporting global regulatory diversity.

This document defines the privacy and data minimization principles that govern ORF implementations. It is a normative companion to the ORF Specification and is incorporated by reference.

Key Principle: ORF explicitly recognizes that a valid receipt does not require identification of a natural person.

Design Philosophy

ORF privacy design is guided by the following principles:

1

Data minimization by default

Collect only what is necessary for the transaction

2

Optional identity, not assumed identity

Personal identification is always optional unless legally required

3

Separation of trust from identity

Receipt authenticity doesn't depend on personal identification

4

Jurisdictional compliance without over-collection

Meet legal requirements without unnecessary data gathering

5

Portability and user control

Users should control their receipt data and identity

Scope: ORF is intended to function in environments ranging from anonymous cash transactions to highly regulated B2B invoicing systems.

3.2 Functional Identifiers

MUST NOT directly identify a natural person

  • ‚Ä¢ Receipt IDs
  • ‚Ä¢ Loyalty IDs
  • ‚Ä¢ Warranty references
  • ‚Ä¢ Visit or session identifiers

3.3 Personal Data

Optional and Restricted

Information that identifies or can reasonably identify a natural person, including:

  • ‚Ä¢ Legal names
  • ‚Ä¢ Government-issued identifiers
  • ‚Ä¢ Tax identification numbers associated with individuals

Customer Identity Models

ORF defines three supported customer identity models.

P0 ‚Äì Anonymous (Default)

No customer object is present

This model SHOULD be used for:

  • ‚Ä¢ Retail purchases
  • ‚Ä¢ Cash transactions
  • ‚Ä¢ Wallet-based receipts
  • ‚Ä¢ Any scenario without a legal identification requirement

P1 ‚Äì Pseudonymous

A customer object MAY be present containing only functional identifiers

Examples:

  • ‚Ä¢ Loyalty programs
  • ‚Ä¢ Returns and warranties
  • ‚Ä¢ Appointment or visit references

Note: No personal data is included.

P2 ‚Äì Identified

Customer-identifying data is present

This model MUST only be used when:

  • ‚Ä¢ Legally required (e.g., VAT invoices, national fiscal systems)
  • ‚Ä¢ Explicitly requested or consented to by the customer

Data Minimization Rules

ORF implementations MUST adhere to the following rules:

  1. 1
    Personal data MUST NOT be included unless required or consented to.

    Default to P0 (Anonymous) unless there's a legal requirement or explicit customer consent.

  2. 2
    Only the minimum necessary personal data MAY be included.

    If personal data is required, include only what's legally mandated or consented to.

  3. 3
    Optional fields MUST NOT be repurposed to infer identity.

    Don't use seemingly neutral fields like addresses or phone numbers to identify individuals when not required.

  4. 4
    Implementers SHOULD prefer functional identifiers over personal data.

    Use receipt IDs, loyalty program numbers, or other non-personal references when possible.

Important Requirement

Receipts SHOULD remain valid and verifiable in the absence of any customer data.

Jurisdictional Overrides

Certain jurisdictions require customer identification for specific transaction types.

Legal Compliance

In such cases:

  • ‚Ä¢ ORF allows identification through jurisdiction-specific extensions
  • ‚Ä¢ Identification requirements MUST be limited to the applicable scope
  • ‚Ä¢ Implementers SHOULD document the legal basis for inclusion

Key Principle: ORF does not impose additional identification beyond jurisdictional mandates.

Receipt Portability and Wallets

ORF supports portable, user-controlled digital receipts.

Wallet Implementation Requirements

  • ‚Ä¢ SHOULD support anonymous and pseudonymous receipts
  • ‚Ä¢ SHOULD allow users to manage visibility of personal data
  • ‚Ä¢ MUST NOT require identification to store or display receipts

Portability MUST NOT be conditioned on identity disclosure.

Out of Scope (Non-Normative)

The following are explicitly out of scope for this document:

  • ‚Ä¢ Marketing analytics, behavioral profiling, or advertising use of receipt data
  • ‚Ä¢ Cross-merchant or cross-platform customer tracking
  • ‚Ä¢ Identity resolution, enrichment, or data brokerage
  • ‚Ä¢ Consent management user interfaces
  • ‚Ä¢ Legal interpretation or enforcement of privacy regulations

Note: ORF does not standardize downstream data use beyond receipt issuance, storage, and verification.

Non-Normative Examples

Example A: Anonymous Retail Receipt (P0)

A cash purchase where no customer object is present and the receipt contains only transactional data.

Example B: Pseudonymous Warranty Receipt (P1)

A receipt containing a customer reference ID used solely for warranty lookup, without any personal identifiers.

Example C: VAT Invoice with Identified Customer (P2)

A receipt including customer legal name and VAT ID, required to support tax credit claims under EU VAT rules.

Note: These examples are illustrative and do not impose additional requirements.

Relationship to Data Protection Law (Informative)

ORF is designed to be compatible with common data protection frameworks, including GDPR, CCPA/CPRA, and similar regimes.

Summary
